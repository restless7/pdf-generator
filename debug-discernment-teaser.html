We consume more information daily than a person in the 15th century encountered in a lifetime. Yet our brains still operate with the same hardware — a System 1 that craves speed and a System 2 that requires deliberate effort.

This asymmetry between our biological processing and our digital environment creates a vulnerability that is aggressively exploited by political actors, advertisers, and algorithmic systems designed to maximize engagement over truth.

The Six Cognitive Biases That Shape Your Reality

Confirmation Bias

We instinctively seek information that confirms our existing beliefs, creating invisible walls around our worldview.

Anchoring Effect

The first piece of information we encounter disproportionately influences all subsequent judgments — even when it's wrong.

Availability Heuristic

We overestimate the probability of events that come easily to mind, typically those that are recent, dramatic, or emotionally charged.

The Sharing Decision Matrix

Before amplifying any information, run it through these four checkpoints:

  • ✓ Have I verified this information through at least two independent, credible sources?
  • ✓ Am I sharing this because it is true, or because it confirms what I already believe?
  • ✓ Could sharing this cause harm to a person, group, or institution — even if it's technically correct?
  • ✓ Would I be comfortable defending this information in a professional or academic setting?
  • ✓ Am I emotionally triggered right now? If yes, I should wait 24 hours before sharing.

The Truth Sandwich Method

When correcting misinformation, never lead with the myth. Instead, use this evidence-based structure:

Step 1

Lead with the Truth

Start with the verified fact, clearly and simply stated.

Step 2

Mention the Myth

Briefly reference the false claim — but only after establishing the truth.

Step 3

Explain the Fallacy

Clarify why the myth is wrong and what mechanism makes it persuasive.

Step 4

Reinforce the Truth

Close by restating the verified fact with supporting evidence.
